# Jobs-to-be-Done Framework for Audience Research

> Reference for building the JTBD section of buyer personas. Load when building personas at L1+ context.

---

## The Core Insight

People don't buy products. They hire them to do a job. Understanding the job — not the demographics — is what makes personas actionable.

A 45-year-old suburban dad and a 22-year-old college student might both "hire" the same meal delivery app for the same job: "Feed me something I trust is halal without me having to research it." The demographics differ; the job is identical.

---

## The Three Job Types

### Functional Job
The practical task they need accomplished.

**Questions to surface it:**
- What are they literally trying to get done?
- What triggers the search for a solution?
- What does "done" look like?

**Example:** "I need to order halal food delivered to my house tonight."

### Emotional Job
How they want to feel during and after using the product.

**Questions to surface it:**
- What anxiety does this remove?
- What confidence does this give?
- How do they feel when the job is done well vs. poorly?

**Example:** "I want to feel confident that my family is eating halal food without the mental load of verifying every ingredient."

### Social Job
How they want to be perceived by others.

**Questions to surface it:**
- What would they tell friends about using this?
- Does using this product say something about who they are?
- Would they share or hide that they use this?

**Example:** "I want my friends to know I found a reliable halal delivery option so they can use it too."

## Forces of Progress

Four forces determine whether someone will switch to your product:

### Push (from current situation)
What's painful about how they solve this today?
- "I spend 30 minutes checking ingredients on DoorDash every night"
- "I drive 20 minutes to the only halal restaurant near me"

### Pull (toward new solution)
What's attractive about the alternative?
- "One tap ordering with verified halal"
- "Delivered in 30 minutes, already checked"

### Anxiety (about switching)
What fears stop them from trying something new?
- "What if their halal certification isn't real?"
- "What if the food quality is bad?"

### Habit (keeping them where they are)
What's comfortable about the current solution?
- "I already know the DoorDash app"
- "I trust the one restaurant I always go to"

**The switch happens when Push + Pull > Anxiety + Habit.**

Understanding these four forces for each persona makes objection handling and messaging much sharper.

## Job Mapping Template

For each persona, map:

```
JOB: [One sentence — what they're trying to accomplish]

Functional: [The practical task]
Emotional:  [How they want to feel]
Social:     [How they want to be perceived]

Current solution: [How they solve it today]
Push:    [What's painful about current solution]
Pull:    [What's attractive about yours]
Anxiety: [What fears stop the switch]
Habit:   [What keeps them where they are]

Hiring criteria: [What matters most when choosing]
  1. [Most important factor]
  2. [Second most important]
  3. [Third most important]

Firing criteria: [What would make them stop using you]
  1. [Deal-breaker]
  2. [Serious annoyance]
```

## Common Mistakes

- **Too many jobs** — Each persona should have one primary job. If you've listed 5 functional jobs, you probably have 5 personas, not 1.
- **Feature-level jobs** — "I need a mobile app" isn't a job. "I need to order food while commuting" is. Jobs are outcome-level, not solution-level.
- **Demographic jobs** — "As a 35-year-old woman" isn't a job trigger. Age doesn't create demand; situations do. Focus on the circumstance that triggers the search.
- **Ignoring social jobs** — For many products (especially B2B and consumer brands), the social job is the strongest buying motivator but the least asked about.
