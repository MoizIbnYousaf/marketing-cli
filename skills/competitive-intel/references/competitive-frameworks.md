# Competitive Analysis Frameworks

> Reference for structuring competitive intelligence. Load when running Deep Teardown or Gap Finder modes.

## Porter's Five Forces (Adapted for Messaging)

The classic strategy framework, adapted for competitive messaging analysis rather than pure business strategy.

### 1. Competitive Rivalry
How intense is the messaging competition?

- How many competitors make similar claims?
- Is the market converging on identical messaging ("all-in-one", "AI-powered")?
- Are competitors competing on price, features, or positioning?

**Messaging implication:** High rivalry = need for mechanism-based differentiation. Simple claims won't cut it.

### 2. Threat of New Entrants
How easy is it for new competitors to enter and claim your positioning?

- Is your differentiation defensible or easily copied?
- Do new entrants need significant proof/credibility to compete?
- Are there positioning moats (brand recognition, proprietary data, community)?

**Messaging implication:** If entry is easy, your positioning needs a mechanism or proof that's hard to replicate.

### 3. Threat of Substitutes
What alternatives could replace your entire category?

- DIY solutions (spreadsheets, manual processes)
- Hiring a person (consultant, agency, freelancer)
- Different category entirely (different approach to the same problem)
- Doing nothing (living with the pain)

**Messaging implication:** Your strongest competitor might not be in your category at all. Position against the real alternative.

### 4. Buyer Power
How much leverage do buyers have?

- Can they easily switch between competitors?
- Are they price-sensitive or value-driven?
- Do they have access to comparison information?

**Messaging implication:** High buyer power = need for strong differentiation and risk reversal. Low switching costs mean you need to earn loyalty through positioning, not lock-in.

### 5. Supplier Power (less relevant for messaging)
Skip this for most messaging-focused analysis. Only relevant when your supply chain affects positioning (e.g., "We're the only service with [exclusive partnership]").

## Messaging Teardown Checklist

When analyzing a competitor's messaging, capture these elements systematically:

### Homepage (30 seconds)
- [ ] Headline — what's the primary claim?
- [ ] Sub-headline — how do they expand on it?
- [ ] Hero CTA — what action do they want?
- [ ] Social proof — what proof appears above the fold?
- [ ] Visual tone — professional, casual, playful, corporate?

### Value Proposition (2 minutes)
- [ ] Core transformation promised
- [ ] Primary mechanism (how they deliver results)
- [ ] Target audience signaling (who is this for?)
- [ ] Key differentiator from alternatives

### Pricing Page (1 minute)
- [ ] Pricing model (subscription, one-time, freemium, enterprise)
- [ ] Tier names (what do they call their plans?)
- [ ] What gates the upgrade (features, usage, support?)
- [ ] Anchor pricing (do they show a high price to make others look reasonable?)

### Proof & Trust (2 minutes)
- [ ] Testimonial format (quotes, case studies, logos, numbers)
- [ ] Specificity of proof ("10,000 customers" vs. "Sarah from Ohio saved $5,000")
- [ ] Authority markers (press logos, certifications, partnerships)
- [ ] Risk reversal (guarantees, free trials, refund policies)

### Content & Distribution (2 minutes)
- [ ] Blog topics and frequency
- [ ] Social platform presence and activity
- [ ] Email capture strategy (lead magnets, newsletter)
- [ ] SEO focus (what keywords do they target?)

## 2x2 Positioning Map Templates

Choose the axes that reveal the most meaningful gaps for your market:

### Common Axis Pairs

**Simple/Complex x Affordable/Premium**
Good for: Markets with both DIY tools and enterprise solutions

**Specialized/General x Self-serve/High-touch**
Good for: SaaS and service markets

**Speed/Thoroughness x Individual/Team**
Good for: Productivity and workflow tools

**Technical/Non-technical x Automation/Manual**
Good for: Developer tools and no-code markets

### How to Use the Map

1. Plot all competitors on the 2x2
2. Identify the empty quadrant — that's your potential white space
3. Verify the empty quadrant isn't empty for a reason (no demand there)
4. If the empty quadrant has demand, that's your positioning opportunity

**The anti-positioning test:** For each competitor, write the opposite of their positioning. If the opposite is appealing and unclaimed, it's a viable angle.

## Gap Taxonomy

### Messaging Gaps (what nobody says)
- Claims true for you but no competitor makes
- Audiences nobody specifically addresses
- Pain points nobody directly names
- Emotional outcomes nobody promises

### Positioning Gaps (where nobody lives)
- Empty quadrants on the 2x2 map
- Unclaimed market sophistication stages
- Identity positions nobody owns ("the X for Y people")

### Channel Gaps (where nobody shows up)
- Platforms the audience uses but competitors ignore
- Content formats nobody produces (video, podcast, tools)
- Communities nobody engages with

### Pricing Gaps (where nobody charges)
- Price points between existing tiers
- Models nobody offers (lifetime, pay-per-use, free tier)
- Packaging innovations (bundles, unbundles)

### Proof Gaps (what nobody proves)
- Results nobody quantifies
- Use cases nobody documents
- Audiences nobody showcases in case studies
